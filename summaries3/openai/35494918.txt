Staphylococcus aureus is a bacterium known for causing infections, including methicillin-resistant strains like MRSA and methicillin-susceptible strains like MSSA. MSSA can lead to serious infections and is common among the population. Risk factors for MSSA and MRSA infections are similar and can include conditions like immunosuppression, organ transplantation, cancer, dialysis dependence, and diabetes. A case study highlighted a rare instance of disseminated MSSA infection without infective endocarditis in a 48-year-old male with underlying health conditions. The patient presented with various symptoms, leading to a diagnosis of metastatic Staphylococcus aureus infection involving multiple organs. Treatment involved antibiotics, drainage procedures, and multidisciplinary care, resulting in the patient's improvement and transfer to a rehabilitation center. Metastatic Staphylococcus aureus infections are rare but can lead to serious complications, requiring early recognition, appropriate treatment, and source control for better outcomes. Recommended treatments for MSSA infections include penicillinase-stable beta-lactam antibiotics like nafcillin and cloxacillin, with cefazolin and vancomycin as alternatives.
